  • The purpose of Spiritual Disciplines
  • Central Idea of Submission
    These disciplines have laid a solid foundation to prepare us for the next discipline in our study, the discipline of submission. Submission is “the ability to lay down the terrible burden of always needing to get our own way” (Foster, Celebration, p. 111). One of the greatest forms of slavery and frustration experienced by humankind is the obsession to think that things must go the way we want them to go! The discipline of submission helps us to realize that most things in life are not as important as we make them to be. We learn to realize that life goes on and it is okay that we can’t control it.

  • Why we worship
    • The object of worship is God
    • Worship begins with the right attitude and follows with right actions. It is your intentional response to the love that God has shown you through Jesus (Romans 12:1).
    • It is not for the timid or comfortable.
    • It involves opening yourself to living according to the will of God as revealed through His Holy Spirit (attitude).
    • It is obedience (action). It is not an anesthetic, opiate, or escape from the pressing needs of this present life.
  • Preparation for worship and fruit worship
    • The preparation for worship involves gathering to corporate worship with a holy expectancy that stems from daily practicing the presence of God. (Acts 4:31, 9:31,1 Thessalonians 5:16-18)
    • The fruit of worship is the ability to hear the call of God to service (Isaiah 6:5-8)

  • Why is celebration last
    • In fact, the motivating factor to begin most disciplines (spiritual or otherwise) is usually that we know that joy is the end result.
    • Obedience, born out of trust, is the only thing that produces genuine joy. Therefore, joy is a result of the Spiritual Disciplines functioning in our lives. Warning: Don’t try to come up with joy too soon!
    • “It is important to avoid the kind of celebrations that really celebrate nothing. Worse yet is to pretend to celebrate when the spirit of celebration is not in us” (Foster, Celebration, p. 193).
    • “To miss out on joy is to miss out on the reason for your existence” (Lewis Smedes, in The Life, p. 68).
